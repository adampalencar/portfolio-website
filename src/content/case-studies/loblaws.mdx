## Steady Traffic, Seventeen Percent Fewer Buyers

Loblaws is one of Canada's largest grocery retailers. Their website traffic held steady after a redesign of the digital buy flow. Online sales dropped 17% in two months. Same visitors, fewer purchases. The friction was somewhere in the flow.

This was an academic project. Our team of three designed a 5-phase, 90-day research plan to diagnose the cause. We executed the first three phases: stakeholder interviews, heuristic evaluation, and competitive analysis. The remaining phases, usability testing and A/B testing, were designed with full screeners and test plans but never run with real users. **The deliverable was a diagnosis with testable recommendations, not a redesigned checkout.**

## I Ran the Heuristic Evaluation Before We Recruited Anyone

I led the heuristic evaluation against Nielsen's 10 usability principles. The goal was to build a map of specific, testable hypotheses before spending time and budget on participant recruitment. Expert review is fast and cheap. Usability testing is slow and expensive. **Running heuristics first meant every test session could target a documented issue instead of fishing for problems.**

The checkout button hit two heuristics at once: Visibility of System Status and Consistency and Standards. A button that looks disabled when it isn't stops users from clicking. Users who don't click don't buy. The cart loss after registration hit Error Prevention. A user who spends 10 minutes filling a cart, signs up for an account, and returns to an empty basket is not going to start over.

<div class="cs-callout">

**Research decision:** I used heuristic evaluation to generate hypotheses, not conclusions. Expert opinion is fast and specific, but it is not user evidence. Every finding became a testable question for the usability testing phase. The alternative was to go straight to usability testing, but without a map of suspected issues, those sessions would have been exploratory instead of targeted.

</div>

## Three Grocery Sites, One Clear Gap on Mobile

I co-led the competitive analysis against Walmart and Costco across desktop and mobile. The question was not "what are competitors doing better?" but "what are shoppers already trained to expect?"

Online grocery shoppers in Canada split time across these retailers. Walmart had set the bar: card-based navigation, clear calls to action, consistent button styling, integrated login. **Loblaws' mobile experience was functional but felt dense by comparison.** Costco had the same density problems as Loblaws: long lists, cluttered menus, navigation buried under promotional content.

**What I chose not to do:** I excluded No Frills from the competitive set because it runs on the same Loblaws website codebase. I excluded Longos because it operates only in Ontario, making it an invalid national comparison. Two competitors doing the same thing does not make it correct, so I used the comparison to identify where Loblaws fell below established patterns, not to copy solutions.

## Every A/B Scenario Started With a Finding

I designed 4 of the 10 A/B testing scenarios, including the strongest one: the $30 minimum cart requirement. The heuristic evaluation found that users only discovered this threshold after clicking the cart dropdown. By then, users who didn't want to spend $30 had already invested time adding items. That is cart abandonment built into the flow.

- **Variation A (current):** Minimum threshold messaging appears only in the cart dropdown after clicking.
- **Variation B:** Immediate messaging on the item page when items total below $30, with a clear explanation of how much more is needed.

**These scenarios were designed but never ran.** If executed, each had a KPI tied to it: cart abandonment for the threshold test, checkout completion for guest checkout, feature usage for meal planner visibility. The screeners are written, test tasks defined, and A/B variations have implementation instructions. What they need is users.

## The Map Before the Territory

This project taught me how much structure matters before any user sits down to test. The heuristic evaluation and competitive analysis gave me a map of the problem space in 2 weeks. Without that map, usability testing would have been exploratory instead of targeted.

I am confident in the diagnosis because the heuristic findings are specific and reproducible. But until someone watches a real user struggle through the checkout, the recommendations remain educated predictions. What I would do differently is push to run at least 3 of the A/B scenarios during the project timeline instead of delivering all 10 as a plan. Three validated findings carry more weight than ten untested hypotheses.

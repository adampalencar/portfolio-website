## One Screen. Four Systems. Nothing Hidden.

<p class="cs-summary-line">Four competing information systems on one screen, designed so nothing hides and nothing scrolls.</p>

Orbital Concierge is a puzzle game where you route one elevator through a building. Every stop costs you a move from a shrinking budget, passengers lose patience with each detour, and the car only holds a limited number. **All 4 systems share a single screen.** Nothing scrolls, nothing tabs, nothing hides.

I designed and built all 24 levels in Godot 4.5, then ran 3 rounds of structured playtesting. All testers understood the core loop in their first session. One called the results screen animation something that "makes my spine tingle." Another kept the game open to listen to the music while writing feedback.

But I had blind spots: **scoring was invisible during play**, tutorial hints vanished after firing once, and 2 of 3 testers couldn't find the restart button. I closed every gap within 2 weeks through thoughtful and tested design.

## Small Events Stay Quiet. Permanent Ones Get Loud.

<p class="cs-summary-line">Feedback intensity proportional to player cost. Quiet by default, layered when permanent.</p>

Every feedback element on a tight screen costs space. **I matched the response to the cost.**

A floor button hover slides 6px right with a back-ease bounce. Small, confirms the button is alive. A successful delivery gets a floating score popup and a softened chime. Low-cost events, proportional response.

Losing a passenger is permanent. The feedback has to communicate that. The manifest border turns red, a warning triangle appears, text colour shifts, and the screen shakes. **Each layer catches something the others might miss.** Colour alone is easy to overlook mid-puzzle, especially if the player is colourblind, so the shake grabs attention and the icon makes the state explicit.

<Image src={criticalStateImg} alt="Cargo at zero tolerance: red border, warning triangle, text color shift" />

The results screen reveals each delivery 1 at a time, bonuses individually, score ticker counting up with rising pitch before stars bounce in. Players process each piece before the final rating lands.

<Image src={resultsScreenImg} alt="Results screen with delivery breakdown, loss penalty, bonuses, 2-star rating" />

<div class="cs-callout">

**Design decision:** I never added toast notifications, particle effects, or celebration on routine deliveries. The screen escalates only when consequences do.

</div>

## Playtesting Fixed What I Couldn't See From the Inside

<p class="cs-summary-line">3 testers, 4 structured prompts each, every gap closed in 2 weeks.</p>

The game worked. The interface had communication gaps that only showed up when real players sat down.

<div class="cs-callout">

The scoring guide was a paragraph of rules. Nobody opened it.

</div>

I replaced it with a 3-column table: passenger type, base score, patience bonus formula. Scannable in seconds.

## What's Left

<p class="cs-summary-line">Honest constraints and what comes next.</p>

The single screen constraint forced a clean layout where nothing got space it didn't earn. It also made accessibility harder.

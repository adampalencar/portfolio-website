## One brand, asked to do a whole business's job

Meristem is a self-initiated concept. I invented a fictional B2B wholesale plant nursery so I could design a complete brand and then prove it holds in every place the business actually lives. Nothing here is a real client. The point was to build one identity and carry it, without a seam, from a printed hang-tag to a working website to a month of planned social.

It supplies the trade: landscapers, interior plantscapers, florists, and garden centres who buy by grade and pot size and reorder every week. That reader shaped the whole thing, from a printed hang-tag to a website you order from. This is a business you place an order with on a Monday morning, not a lifestyle brand you admire on a shelf.

## The category runs on spreadsheets

Wholesale nurseries grow beautiful stock and sell it through PDFs. Availability goes out as an emailed spreadsheet, the website is a dated brochure with no way to order, and the brand is a logo if it exists at all. The gap is not taste, it is function. A buyer cannot see what is ready, in what grade, right now.

<div class="cs-callout">

**The idea:** one thing carried everywhere. Honest, current availability, designed to be read and acted on. The brand is the system, not the logo.

## One identity, from the masthead to the plant tag

Before a pixel of website, I built the system underneath it: a mark, a fixed palette, three type roles, and rules strict enough to survive a one-colour plant tag. The mark is a seedling, adapted from the Phosphor icon set, because a *meristem* is the growth tip where a plant makes new tissue. I kept the botanical language flat and geometric so the identity reads as design, not decoration.

## Getting to a mark that survives a plant tag

The seedling did not arrive first. I drew a mark by hand and it read as a trident. I tried a purely geometric node and lost the plant. A detailed leaf looked right on screen but would clog at tag size in one colour. The final mark is adapted from the Phosphor set for one reason: it stays a legible seedling at 24 pixels, whether it is on a screen or stamped on a hang-tag.

## A website the trade can order from

The homepage is the centre of the piece, because a supplier's site is where the money moves. The hero states the offer, and this week's stock sits one tap away in a preview card. The real work is the ledger below it.

<figure class="cs-figure cs-figure--full">
  <Image src={homepageImg} alt="Meristem homepage: text navigation with a trade-account call to action, the Plants properly supplied hero, and a floating This Week preview card listing three lots with status chips and a link to the full list" />
  <figcaption class="cs-figure__caption">Homepage &middot; the offer up top, this week's stock one tap from the full list</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure class="cs-figure cs-figure--full">
  <Image src={availabilityLedgerImg} alt="Meristem availability ledger: a filterable trade list with category chips, and rows carrying botanical and common names, pot size, height, grade, quantity price breaks, and colour-coded status chips for available, limited, and booking" />
  <figcaption class="cs-figure__caption">Availability ledger &middot; the ordering tool, not a catalogue you look at</figcaption>
</figure>

Every row carries the data a buyer actually decides on: botanical and common name, pot size, height, grade, a quantity price break, and a status. Available, Limited, and Booking each get their own colour and their own action, so a sold-out lot offers to book the next run instead of pretending it can ship.

<figure class="cs-figure cs-figure--full">
  <Image src={interactionImg} alt="Availability interaction states: a row on hover surfaces an Add button, an added row shows a terracotta rail with a quantity stepper set to 24, and the module foot reconciles a running one-lot, twenty-four-unit order total" />
  <figcaption class="cs-figure__caption">Interaction &middot; hover to add, commit with a stepper, watch the order total tally</figcaption>
</figure>

The interaction is where the UX argument lands. Hover a row and the add control appears. Add a lot and it commits with a terracotta rail and a quantity stepper, while the foot of the module keeps a running order total. Building an order feels like ticking down a delivery sheet, which is exactly how this buyer already works.

<figure class="cs-figure cs-figure--full">
  <Image src={categoriesImg} alt="Categories band: six browse cards using the identity glyphs for Foliage, Perennials, Trees and Shrubs, Ornamental Grasses, Succulents and Cacti, and Groundcover, each with a short description and a view availability link" />
  <figcaption class="cs-figure__caption">Category cards &middot; the same glyphs from the identity, one build not two</figcaption>

Real text navigation instead of image buttons, category cards that reuse the identity glyphs one-to-one, and a footer carrying the EN and FR toggle a Canadian trade supplier needs. The identity system and the website are one build, not a logo handed to a separate site.

## What I set out to prove

Meristem starts as a brand and ends as something you can place an order with. One system runs from the plant tag through the identity into a website built to take a wholesale order, without cracking anywhere along the way. The same system extends to print collateral and a running content channel, which I have built and run for real clients, so here I kept the concept to the parts it proves best: the identity and the site. It is drawn entirely from scratch and stays honest about what a concept can and cannot claim. If I ran it for real, the first thing I would watch is whether that availability ledger actually turns site visits into opened trade accounts, and I would tune the system from there.
